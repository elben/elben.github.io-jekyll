A story. A fairytale. The whole experience, a delight to the senses. That’s what Dave was building. The idea came to him in fourth period English yesterday. Mr. Kosh had read them a nice story about a man and his little garden below a hill, and how he slowly built his garden, adding vineyards and trees and carved lakes and rivers, and how after twenty years the man looked up and found himself forever lost in his own forest.

After that story, Dave wanted to make something beautiful. It was for Katie, a girl. They shared two classes, math in the morning and English in the afternoon. When Mr. Kosh finished the story Dave had looked over at Katie and he saw her so alive and beautiful that it hurt him.

After school that day Dave did not talk on the bus the whole way back. He stared out the window and did not notice when his best friend Brandon got up to sit next to someone else. Dave was thinking, iterating, building. This was his largest project yet, and it would be his best.

He started the project that weekend. He walked around the house and collected little pieces of paper, cloth and ribbons. His mother took him to local art store, and there he slowly walked the isles, gliding his young fingers along the textured papers and the smooth glass trinkets. He took in how the papers felt and how light bounced and traveled through the glass. He took it all in, these little hints of beauty. And he thought of Katie, how nice it would be to touch her cheeks and to get lost in her eyes. He walked out of the store without buying anything for he had no money to spend, but that did not matter to him. He didn’t even know what he wanted to build yet. He knew only the passion that drove him, and that was enough.

Next Saturday his best friend Brandon asked if he wanted to ride bikes. “I can’t,” Dave replied, “my mom said I can’t.” It was a lie, but did he have a choice?

“Then let’s play on your computer,” Brandon said.

“I can’t have friends over.”

Brandon looked at him, and saw. “You’re hiding something.”

“What?”

“You’re hiding something. I know it.”

“Well it’s not my fault if—.” Dave almost blurted it out, that he doesn’t care about anything but building this something for Katie, that all other wonderments and enjoyments meant nothing to him, and will never mean anything again.

“Fine, don’t tell me,” Brandon shot as he got on his bike to leave.

Dave watched Brandon pedal back home, along quiet street they shared. He looked up and noticed the steady dance of autumn leaves. A feeling came over Dave, and it surprised him. He felt as if nothing would ever be the same, that he would never find a way out of this forest he was building.
