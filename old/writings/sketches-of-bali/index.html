Sketches of Bali

Elizabeth was tired of the tourists and had found a family of eight who had chartered a van from Denpasar to Bukti. She had given each child a piece of chocolate and the father 5,000 rupiah. So she went with them to Bukti, wherever that was. She sat in the back with the children and played with them in broken Indonesian. They had just passed a small township when the driver stopped to pee, so Elizabeth and the children popped the back hatch and wriggled out and ran around and bought young coconuts from an old man pulling a wagon piled twice his height. He used a large, rusty cleaver to split the top off the coconuts and pulled out straws from his thrice-stitched pockets and offered them to Elizabeth. She looked at his ancient hands, took the straws and smiled. Saya suka kepala disini—I like the head here—she said. They all laughed, and the children ran around singing kepala kelapa, kepala kelapa, Eli adalah kepala kelapa!—Coconut head, coconut head, Eli is a coconut head! When the driver walked out of the woods (zipping up his pants), Elizabeth and the children said goodbye to the old man and his wagon and piled back into the van.

The adults up front started talking about Suharto and Elizabeth strained to hear and wanted to chime in because she had studied international politics and had some ideas of her own. But they were talking too fast and she couldn’t keep up, so she rolled down her windows and let the breeze sneak into the van, whistling that ancient Eastern song which slowly lulled the children to sleep. Elizabeth stared out the window and liked how the van rolled steadily down the moistened dirt road, wiping out the layered history of previous motorcars. Motorcar after motorcar, layer after layer—all trampling the trampled. Elizabeth liked that thought, so she kept it.

Beyond the road tin shacks glittered under cool sunlight. A weary red shack caught Elizabeth’s eye, and she noticed a bucket painted blue placed below its slanted roof capturing the slow drip, drip, drip of the morning rain. Clothing lines shot from the red shack and tied themselves to neighboring shacks, and she followed the lines from shack to shack as the hanging clothes waved hello and goodbye in the wind. The van tumbled on, and at the last possible moment before the sight disappeared she realized that the lines formed a web, a network—a village.

Suddenly Elizabeth felt her body jerk forward and she was flying and her face slammed into the seats in front of her. The children woke up and the adults got out of the van. She looked up to a donkey staring at her. He was a gray working donkey with a patch around its left eye. A satchel hung from each side of the donkey, and a small boy and his mother were trying to get the donkey off the road. The mother was urging the donkey with a stick and yelled something to the boy. The boy ran off and came back with a bushel of bananas and a large banana leaf. He waved the bananas in front of the donkey and when that didn’t work he tried to tickle the donkey with the banana leaf, yelling bergerak, bodoh! The boy eventually gave up, grabbed a stick and followed his mother’s lead. The adults and children emptied the van and they all had sticks and bananas but the donkey would not budge. Elizabeth laughed and closed her eyes and took a deep breath and searched for the smell of the dirt road and bananas and donkey. Bergerak, bodoh. Bergerak! She opened her eyes and up ahead the road dipped and disappeared. Instead she saw kites made out of newspapers with colorful tails carefully cut out from plastic market bags. The kites danced in the wind, laughing.
